Most of the proteins in the CADASIL plasma signature have obvious biological connections to vascular disease, white matter pathology, or neuroinflammation. TARDBP — which encodes TDP-43 — is an outlier. TDP-43 is best known as the pathological aggregating protein in ALS and FTD-TDP (frontotemporal dementia with TDP-43 inclusions), and its presence in the late-stage CADASIL plasma signature is flagged by Keller et al. as having a "specific CADASIL role yet to be investigated."

Why would a TDP-43-encoding gene show up in a vascular dementia plasma signature? Several possibilities exist. First, TDP-43 pathology is increasingly recognized as a co-pathology in aging brains and in vascular dementia — it is not unique to ALS/FTD. Second, chronic vascular ischemia could stress neurons and glia in ways that promote TDP-43 mislocalization from the nucleus to the cytoplasm, a precursor to aggregation. Third, the plasma elevation could reflect cell death (TDP-43 released from dying cells) rather than TDP-43 pathology per se.

This is marked as an OPEN claim because none of these mechanisms have been established in CADASIL specifically. The finding is hypothesis-generating, not hypothesis-confirming. However, it is a meaningful direction: if TDP-43 pathology contributes to CADASIL dementia, it would imply a shared mechanism with FTD and ALS, and could connect CADASIL to the broader TDP-43 proteinopathy literature.

For the VascBrain program, this suggests a post-hoc analysis: do CADASIL patients with elevated plasma TARDBP-associated proteins show a more rapidly progressive dementia syndrome? Do they show any neuropathological TDP-43 inclusions if autopsy data are available? The claim cannot be closed until mechanistic or neuropathological evidence is available.
