Early-stage CADASIL is conventionally described as a vascular disease — NOTCH3 aggregates accumulate, arteriolar smooth muscle cells degrade, white matter begins to suffer. What the Keller 2024 plasma proteomics study adds is a metabolic layer to this story: the earliest plasma-detectable signal is not neuroinflammation but a failure of oxidative homeostasis.

The specific pathways enriched in the early-stage 16-protein signature are heme biosynthesis (p=3.3e-5), porphyrin metabolism (p=5.3e-4), and glutathione metabolism (p=9.3e-4). These numbers are not marginal — heme biosynthesis is the top hit by two orders of magnitude compared to any inflammatory pathway. This is striking because heme biosynthesis feeds directly into cytochrome systems that manage reactive oxygen species, and glutathione is the primary cellular antioxidant buffer.

The implication is that before complement activates, before astrocytes and oligodendrocytes show dysfunction (that comes in the late stage, as described in [[CADASIL disease progression involves a cell-type shift from endothelial and fibroblast dysfunction in early stages to astrocyte and oligodendrocyte involvement in late stages]]), the earliest measurable plasma event is oxidative stress and mitochondrial metabolic failure at the vascular wall. The cell-type data for the early signature identifies endothelial cells and fibroblasts as the source — consistent with a primary vascular event driving systemic metabolic distress detectable in plasma.

This matters for biomarker design: if you want to detect CADASIL early, you want an oxidative stress or heme metabolism readout, not a neuroinflammatory one. It also matters for therapy: early intervention windows may be amenable to antioxidant or mitochondria-targeted strategies, before the complement-driven damage of late-stage disease becomes the dominant biology.

This pathway signature is mechanistically distinct from [[complement dysregulation and lipid-peroxisome metabolism are the dominant pathway signals in late-stage CADASIL plasma proteomics]], which defines the late-stage pattern. The two signatures together establish [[plasma proteomics using SomaScan 7k identifies two distinct molecular signatures for early-stage and late-stage CADASIL reflecting stage-specific pathological mechanisms]].
