VEGFR1 undergoes an age-related shift in alternative splicing that increases production of the soluble isoform sVEGFR1 (also known as sFLT1). This soluble decoy receptor binds and sequesters VEGFA without activating downstream angiogenic signaling, effectively suppressing vascular maintenance. In the Torres-Espin 2024 cohort (N=435), no compensatory age-associated increase in VEGFA was observed, suggesting that the VEGF trapping is not offset and results in a net angiogenic deficit.

**Critical nomenclature note:** sVEGFR1 and sFLT1 are the same protein. The vault's [[vascular-contributions-to-neurodegeneration]] goal uses "sFLT1" terminology (from SomaScan proteomics), while this paper uses "VEGFR1/sVEGFR1" (from MSD V-Plex). All claims about sFLT1 apply to sVEGFR1 and vice versa.

This mechanism directly explains [[sflt1-vegf-imbalance-associates-with-reduced-axonogenesis-pathway-activity-in-multi-cohort-somascan-data-after-vascular-comorbidity-and-batch-effect-correction]] -- the age-dependent alternative splicing shift toward sVEGFR1/sFLT1 creates the sFLT1/VEGF imbalance observed in SomaScan data.

Extends [[elevated-sflt1-in-vascbrain-simoa-plasma-correlates-with-cadasil-clinical-severity-and-white-matter-lesion-burden-independently-of-vegf]] -- the decoy receptor mechanism explains why sFLT1 elevation correlates with disease severity: more VEGF trapping means less vascular maintenance.

Connects to [[sflt1-associated-reductions-in-axonogenesis-pathway-scores-reflect-vegf-pathway-suppression-rather-than-direct-sflt1-activity]] -- confirms that sFLT1/sVEGFR1 acts primarily through VEGF sequestration, consistent with indirect pathway suppression.
