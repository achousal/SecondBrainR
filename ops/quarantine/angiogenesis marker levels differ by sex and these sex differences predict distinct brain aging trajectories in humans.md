# angiogenesis marker levels differ by sex and these sex differences predict distinct brain aging trajectories in humans

This is the central thesis of Torres-Espin et al. 2024 (Science Translational Medicine): the vascular endothelial compartment ages differently in women and men, and this divergence is measurable in plasma and predicts divergent brain outcomes. The paper uses a longitudinal cohort of N=435 older adults (mean age 74, 207 female) to show that plasma levels of key angiogenesis regulators -- PlGF, VEGFR1, bFGF, VEGFA-C -- differ by sex in both magnitude and age-trajectory, and that these differences carry downstream consequences for executive function, grey matter volume, and white matter hyperintensity burden.

The mechanistic framing proposes two orthogonal dimensions. The first, "aberrant angiogenesis" (AA), is dominated by PlGF and VEGFR1 (=sFLT1/sVEGFR1 as a VEGFA decoy receptor) and represents dysregulated, pathological angiogenic signaling that increases with age preferentially in women. The second, "vascular health" (VH), is driven by bFGF and represents protective vascular maintenance. Women show steeper age-associated increases in AA and a striking directional reversal around age 75: higher AA associates with better brain outcomes before 75 but worse outcomes after, whereas men show a consistently inverse association at all ages. This is not a subtle modulation -- grey matter volume mediates 48% of the AA-executive function pathway in women but not in men.

Because this note was derived from the paper title before full-text access, it is framed as a preliminary claim capturing the paper's central assertion. Richer evidence claims (specific markers, effect sizes, mediation paths, and methods) are captured in the detailed notes from the full literature note already in the vault.

This claim is the orienting umbrella under which a cluster of more specific evidence claims sit. It situates why sex must be treated as a biological variable in any plasma vascular biomarker analysis for brain aging -- the same circulating profile carries opposite prognostic meaning depending on sex and age.
