Cardiovascular care settings do not routinely screen for cognitive impairment, creating a systematic gap in early detection. French et al. 2025 quantify this gap directly: among 162 adults with asymptomatic cardiovascular disease and no prior dementia diagnosis, 29% scored in the cognitive impairment range on the MoCA when adjusted for age, sex, and education using NACC normative data. Approximately 70% scored below the normative population mean, and 18.5% fell below the -2 SD threshold corresponding to the dementia range.

These prevalence figures substantially exceed general population expectations for cognitively unimpaired adults. The finding is methodologically grounded because the MoCA cutpoints used NACC normative corrections rather than the traditional fixed cutoff of 26, which is known to inflate impairment rates in older or less educated populations. This makes the 29% estimate conservative relative to studies using uncorrected thresholds.

The screening gap operates at a systems level: patients in cardiovascular care pathways are evaluated for vascular risk but not for the cognitive consequences of that vascular burden. This is a missed opportunity because the same vascular pathology that drives atherosclerosis also contributes to cerebral small vessel disease and downstream neurodegeneration, as described in [[endothelial dysfunction blood-brain barrier disruption and neuroinflammation are the three core pathophysiological pathways underlying vascular cognitive impairment and dementia]]. The bidirectional relationship between cardiovascular and neurodegenerative disease -- [[neurodegenerative and cerebrovascular diseases have bidirectional pathogenic interactions mediated through shared vascular mechanisms]] -- means that cardiovascular patients are a mechanistically enriched population for cognitive risk.

The practical consequence is that plasma biomarkers like p-tau217, which achieves AUC 0.94 in this population ([[p-tau217 achieves AUC 0.94 for detecting cognitive impairment in asymptomatic cardiovascular patients]]), could close this screening gap without requiring referral to neurology. The 55% elevated p-tau217 prevalence ([[55 percent of asymptomatic cardiovascular patients show elevated p-tau217 indicating high prevalence of occult tau pathology in a non-AD clinical population]]) further underscores the scale of undetected pathology in this population.
