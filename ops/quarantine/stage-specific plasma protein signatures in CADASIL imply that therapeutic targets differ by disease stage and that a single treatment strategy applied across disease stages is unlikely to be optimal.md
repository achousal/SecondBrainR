One of the most actionable implications of the two-signature finding is therapeutic. If early-stage CADASIL is defined by heme biosynthesis and glutathione disruption, and late-stage CADASIL is defined by complement activation and lipid-peroxisome failure, then the drug targets at each stage are different — and a treatment designed for one stage may be ineffective or even counterproductive at the other.

The Keller 2024 authors state this explicitly: "therapeutic targets may differ based on disease stage." Early-stage targets fall in the oxidative stress and mitochondrial biology space — antioxidants, NRF2 pathway activators, or mitochondria-targeted interventions that address the heme and glutathione pathway failures before they cascade into inflammatory damage. Late-stage targets fall in the complement and neuroinflammation space — complement inhibitors (like those being developed for AMD and other complement-driven diseases) or anti-inflammatory strategies that address the established damage propagation.

This parallels what is known about other progressive neurological diseases: Alzheimer disease interventions targeting amyloid have shown different efficacy profiles in symptomatic versus presymptomatic patients; Parkinson disease neuroprotection trials have repeatedly failed because they were applied too late in the disease course. CADASIL, as a monogenic disease with clear staging criteria, is actually better positioned than sporadic dementias to run stage-stratified trials.

The implication for trial design is concrete: a CADASIL interventional study that does not stratify by disease stage (early versus late based on the proteomics staging or clinical proxies like NIHSS score and white matter lesion load) will mix mechanistically distinct populations, diluting any stage-specific treatment effect and potentially generating false-negative results. This is a design principle that follows directly from [[plasma proteomics using SomaScan 7k identifies two distinct molecular signatures for early-stage and late-stage CADASIL reflecting stage-specific pathological mechanisms]].
