The central interpretive tension in Karvelas et al. is that CADASIL patients do not have significantly larger ePVS volumes than matched controls on average, yet within CADASIL, ePVS volumes are clinically meaningful predictors of disease severity. Two competing explanations are consistent with this pattern. First, ePVS enlargement in CADASIL may reflect a NOTCH3-specific pathomechanism (arteriolar wall thickening and GOM deposits disrupting perivascular fluid dynamics) that only becomes quantitatively distinguishable from normal aging variation at advanced disease stages -- making ePVS a severity marker at high burden but not a case-control discriminator at population mean. Second, ePVS enlargement may be a non-specific consequence of vascular aging that is simply more severe in CADASIL as vascular age accelerates; in this case, ePVS would be tracking vascular age-acceleration rather than anything NOTCH3-specific, analogous to the concern raised in [[plasma simoa biomarker elevations in cadasil are driven by shared vascular comorbidities rather than notch3-specific pathology]].

These two explanations have different implications for therapeutic utility. If ePVS reflects NOTCH3-specific arteriolar dysfunction, then treatment targeting the NOTCH3 pathway (e.g., NOTCH3 cleavage inhibitors or GOM deposit clearance) should reduce ePVS volumes over time, making ePVS a mechanistically appropriate trial endpoint. If ePVS reflects general vascular aging acceleration, it may respond to general vascular risk reduction but would not be specific to NOTCH3-targeted therapy.

Resolving this requires longitudinal ePVS data in CADASIL with concurrent biomarker and genetic stratification (mutation type, EGFR domain) to determine whether ePVS trajectory correlates with NOTCH3-pathway-specific markers rather than general vascular risk factors. The current cross-sectional study cannot adjudicate between these interpretations.

This open question parallels the existing inversion in [[cadasil-translational-characterization]] -- the concern that SIMOA elevations reflect comorbidity burden rather than NOTCH3-specific pathology -- and applies the same logic to the imaging domain.
