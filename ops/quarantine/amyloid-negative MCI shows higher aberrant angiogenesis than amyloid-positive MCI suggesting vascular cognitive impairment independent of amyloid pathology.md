When stratifying by amyloid PET status and cognitive stage (CN-, CN+, MCI-, MCI+), the amyloid-negative MCI group shows significantly higher aberrant angiogenesis (AA) scores than the amyloid-positive MCI group. VEGFR1 mirrors this pattern. bFGF concentrations are higher in cognitively normal versus MCI groups regardless of amyloid status. Cognitively normal groups do not differ in AA by amyloid status.

This dissociation is important: it suggests that angiogenic dysregulation marks a vascular pathway to cognitive impairment that is independent of (and potentially more severe in the absence of) amyloid pathology. The higher AA in amyloid-negative MCI implies these individuals have vascular cognitive impairment without AD, and the angiogenesis markers capture this distinction.

Extends [[cardiovascular patients represent an underscreened population for AD pathology and cognitive impairment warranting systematic biomarker assessment in cardiovascular care settings]] -- angiogenesis markers may identify the vascular fraction of cognitive impairment that AD-specific biomarkers miss.

Connects to [[small vessel disease is the predominant vascular etiology of cognitive impairment and dementia making SVD-targeted biomarkers the primary diagnostic challenge]] -- AA composites could serve as SVD-adjacent biomarkers.

Tension with [[p-tau outperforms amyloid-beta for detecting AD pathology and has stronger concordance with CSF and PET reference standards across independent cohorts]] -- in amyloid-negative MCI, angiogenesis markers outperform AD-specific biomarkers by definition, since the pathology is vascular.
