French et al. (2025) enrolled 162 adults awaiting cardiac or vascular surgery who had no prior dementia diagnosis -- a population referred for cardiovascular indications, not cognitive complaints. Fifty-five percent of these individuals had plasma p-tau217 levels above the positivity threshold. By contrast, only 10.9% showed abnormal amyloid-beta 42/40 ratios. This near fivefold gap between tau and amyloid biomarker positivity rates is the most striking feature of the finding: it implies that a substantial fraction of the tau-elevated patients either carry early-stage tau pathology ahead of amyloid accumulation (incompatible with the amyloid cascade model in its simplest form), or that the p-tau217 threshold is capturing non-AD tau signal elevated by shared vascular mechanisms.

The finding is significant for several reasons. First, it establishes a base rate: roughly one in two asymptomatic cardiovascular patients carries a plasma biomarker signature that would trigger further AD workup if applied in a memory clinic. This is not a marginal elevation -- 55% represents a prevalence that would overwhelm any cascade-referral pathway designed around the assumption that positivity rates are low in non-clinical populations. Second, the discordance with amyloid positivity (55% vs. 10.9%) raises a mechanistic question that the sibling claim on amyloid-beta 42/40 dissociation addresses directly: either the tau elevation precedes or tracks a pathway independent of amyloid.

The cardiovascular setting matters because these patients are systematically excluded from most AD biomarker validation cohorts, which favor memory clinic or research volunteer populations selected partly by the absence of heavy vascular comorbidity. The 55% prevalence figure therefore cannot simply be imported into clinical practice as if it were a community-based screening rate -- the cohort is self-selected by cardiovascular disease severity. Nevertheless, the magnitude is large enough that even substantial selection-effect adjustments would leave a clinically relevant residual prevalence.

This result should be read alongside the parallel finding that [[p-tau217 achieves AUC 0.94 for detecting cognitive impairment in asymptomatic cardiovascular patients]]: the high positivity rate does not merely reflect noise -- the signal is diagnostically organized in the sense that elevated levels discriminate between cognitively impaired and unimpaired individuals within the same cardiovascular cohort. The implication is that the occult tau burden is not uniformly distributed but tracks clinical cognitive status even in this non-referred population.

### Mechanistic tension surfaced

The 55% tau positivity versus 10.9% amyloid positivity creates a mechanistic puzzle. The amyloid cascade hypothesis predicts amyloid precedes tau; if correct, tau positivity should not exceed amyloid positivity in a population-level screen. Two interpretations are coherent: (1) p-tau217 detects very early tau pathology before amyloid thresholds are crossed, which would revise the expected ordering; (2) vascular mechanisms (pericyte loss, BBB breakdown, hypoperfusion) drive tau phosphorylation independently of amyloid cascade, inflating apparent tau positivity in cardiovascular populations. The data at hand cannot distinguish these interpretations because no amyloid PET or CSF data are available from the French et al. cohort to anchor positivity rates to neuropathological ground truth.
