In the Torres-Espin 2024 longitudinal cohort (N=435, median 5 visits), women show greater age-associated increases in the aberrant angiogenesis (AA) composite compared to men. AA is driven by PlGF and VEGFR1 -- both markers of angiogenic dysregulation. This accelerated vascular aging in women aligns with the loss of estrogen-mediated vascular protection after menopause, though the study does not directly measure hormonal status.

The sex-specific AA trajectory has implications for the FCG model work in [[sex-immune-neuroinflammation]]: if gonadal hormones modulate the pace of angiogenic dysregulation (as the menopause-related acceleration suggests), then FCG gonadectomized conditions should show altered AA-equivalent signatures compared to gonadally intact animals.

Connects to [[angiogenesis marker associations with brain structure and cognition are sexually dimorphic with directionality reversing around age 75 in women]] -- the steeper AA trajectory in women explains why the directional reversal occurs earlier and more dramatically in women.

Connects to [[gonads are the dominant but not sole determinant of sex-dimorphic neuroactive steroid levels in the brain]] -- gonadal hormones likely contribute to the sex-dimorphic angiogenic aging trajectory.
