In late-stage CADASIL, the plasma proteomics signal is dominated by complement and lipid-peroxisome disruption. The Keller 2024 SomaScan 7k analysis of 53 late-stage patients identifies complement pathway activation (p=8.0e-5), classical complement cascade (p=1.4e-3), peroxisome pathways (p=2.5e-3), and fatty acid metabolism (p=3.1e-3) as the most enriched disrupted biological processes.

This signature is mechanistically different from the early-stage pattern. Where early-stage CADASIL shows primarily oxidative and heme metabolism disruption (described in [[heme biosynthesis and glutathione metabolism disruption are the dominant pathway signals in early-stage CADASIL plasma proteomics]]), the late-stage signature has shifted to the hallmarks of sustained tissue damage: complement activation, which mediates cell lysis and amplifies inflammation, and lipid-peroxisome dysfunction, which indicates breakdown of myelin and fatty acid homeostasis in white matter.

This distinction is consistent with [[chronic neuroinflammation and microglial activation are central amplifiers in cerebrovascular disease progression not merely downstream consequences]]: by late-stage, what was a primary vascular event has engaged the full inflammatory cascade. Complement is not a trigger but an amplifier — it signals that the vascular damage has been recognized by the innate immune system and that a propagating injury cycle has begun. The peroxisome signal adds a lipid metabolism layer: peroxisomes are critical for the very-long-chain fatty acids in myelin, and their disruption in late-stage disease is consistent with the white matter pathology that clinically defines CADASIL severity at this stage. Notably, the lipid metabolism disruption is not confined to stage-stratified analyses: [[ePVS-associated plasma proteins in CADASIL enrich for leukocyte migration and inflammation pathways while negatively associated proteins enrich for lipid metabolism]] identifies lipid metabolism as the leading negative enrichment signal among ePVS-associated proteins in a parallel SomaScan 7k cohort (Karvelas 2024, N=24), providing imaging-anchored convergence with this late-stage stage-stratified signal and suggesting lipid metabolism disruption is a reproducible feature of CADASIL plasma proteomics across analytic approaches.

The contrast between early and late signatures is the principal scientific claim of [[plasma proteomics using SomaScan 7k identifies two distinct molecular signatures for early-stage and late-stage CADASIL reflecting stage-specific pathological mechanisms]]. The late-stage signature also has the advantage of better external validity: [[the late-stage CADASIL plasma proteomics signature generalizes to an external Colombian validation cohort while the early-stage signature shows only marginal external validity]].
