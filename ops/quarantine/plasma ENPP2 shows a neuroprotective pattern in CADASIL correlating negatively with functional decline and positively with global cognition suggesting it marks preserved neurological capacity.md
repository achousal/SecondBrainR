Most plasma biomarker research in neurodegenerative disease hunts for damage markers — proteins that rise as the brain deteriorates. ENPP2 in CADASIL offers an unusual counterexample: a plasma protein whose pattern tracks neurological preservation rather than decline.

ENPP2 (also known as autotaxin) produces lysophosphatidic acid (LPA), a bioactive lipid mediator with documented roles in oligodendrocyte differentiation, myelination, and neuroprotection in the CNS. In the Keller 2024 cohort, plasma ENPP2 shows the strongest correlations of any protein with clinical outcomes: it correlates negatively with functional decline on the modified Rankin Scale (R=-0.62, p=0.001), negatively with processing speed decline (R=-0.58, p=0.006), and positively with global cognition (R=0.69, p=0.0005). All three correlations are in the direction of protection: more ENPP2 means less functional decline, less speed loss, and better cognition.

This is mechanistically coherent. Single-cell data for the early-stage signature places ENPP2 in the oligodendrocyte lineage. Oligodendrocytes are responsible for myelin maintenance — the key substrate of white matter integrity that CADASIL progressively destroys. If ENPP2 production tracks oligodendrocyte viability and myelin production capacity, then high plasma ENPP2 signals a nervous system that is still maintaining its myelin despite the vascular assault. Patients with higher ENPP2 may be those whose remyelination capacity is intact.

The contrast with [[plasma fibronectin FN1 elevation in CADASIL correlates with brain atrophy and functional decline making it a candidate disease-progression biomarker]] is intentional. FN1 is a damage marker (R=+0.41-0.42 with atrophy and decline). ENPP2 is a resilience marker (R=-0.58 to -0.62 with decline, R=+0.69 with cognition). Together they define a damage-preservation axis that could be used to stratify CADASIL patients not just by stage but by biological resilience capacity — a more nuanced picture than stage alone provides.

The R values here (0.58-0.69) are notably stronger than those for FN1, suggesting ENPP2 may have better signal-to-noise in small cohorts. This makes it a high-priority candidate to test in the VascBrain dataset against the available clinical and imaging outcomes.
