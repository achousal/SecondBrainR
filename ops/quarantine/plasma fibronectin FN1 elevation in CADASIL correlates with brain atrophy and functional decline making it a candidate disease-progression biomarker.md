Among the proteins identified in the CADASIL plasma proteomics signature, fibronectin 1 (FN1) has two properties that elevate it above the others as a progression biomarker candidate. First, it shows statistically significant correlations with clinical outcomes: brain atrophy (R=0.42, p=0.038) and functional decline measured by the modified Rankin Scale (R=0.41, p=0.045). Second, it occupies the highest-connectivity hub position in the protein-protein interaction network for the late-stage signature, with 12 interactions — meaning its elevation is not an isolated finding but a node in the broader network of disease-relevant proteins.

FN1 encodes fibronectin, a glycoprotein that is critical for extracellular matrix assembly and cell adhesion. In vascular disease contexts, fibronectin elevation is associated with vascular remodeling, fibrosis, and endothelial activation. Elevated plasma FN1 in CADASIL could reflect either increased production by stressed endothelial cells and smooth muscle cells, increased shedding from a disrupted vascular wall, or both.

The pairing of FN1 with ENPP2 (discussed in [[plasma ENPP2 shows a neuroprotective pattern in CADASIL correlating negatively with functional decline and positively with global cognition suggesting it marks preserved neurological capacity]]) creates an interesting dual-biomarker contrast: FN1 tracks damage, ENPP2 tracks preservation. This kind of opposing-trajectory design is more informative than a single damage marker because it separates patients who are declining from patients whose cognitive reserve is holding even as structural damage accumulates.

For the VascBrain SomaScan dataset, FN1 is a direct candidate to test against the existing clinical outcome measures (NIHSS, MoCA, imaging markers). The R values of 0.41-0.42 are modest but clinically meaningful, particularly given the small cohort size (N=53 for late-stage) and the difficulty of quantifying progression in a slowly evolving monogenic dementia.
