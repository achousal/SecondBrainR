The relationship between circulating angiogenesis markers and brain aging outcomes depends on both sex and age, with a striking directional reversal in women around age 75. In younger women, higher aberrant angiogenesis (AA) scores associate with better executive function, higher grey matter volume, and lower white matter hyperintensity burden. After approximately age 75, these associations reverse: higher AA predicts worse executive function, lower grey matter volume, and higher WMH in older women. Men show a consistently inverse association between AA and executive function across all ages.

The inflection point at age 75 for executive function (age 71-72 for bFGF-WMH associations) suggests a transition from compensatory to pathological angiogenic signaling. This sex-by-age interaction is critical for interpreting plasma angiogenesis markers in clinical settings -- the same biomarker profile has opposite prognostic meaning depending on sex and age.
