French et al. (2025) enrolled 162 patients from cardiac surgery and vascular surgery waiting lists -- a population with confirmed cardiovascular disease and no prior dementia diagnosis -- and found that [[29 percent of asymptomatic cardiovascular patients have undiagnosed cognitive impairment exceeding general population prevalence and revealing a systematic screening gap|29% met MoCA criteria for cognitive impairment]] and [[55 percent of asymptomatic cardiovascular patients show elevated p-tau217 indicating high prevalence of occult tau pathology in a non-AD clinical population|55% had elevated plasma p-tau217]]. Both findings were clinically unsuspected: patients had not been referred for cognitive evaluation and were attending cardiovascular care settings where brain health assessment is not routine. This co-occurrence of prevalent, undiagnosed cognitive impairment and elevated AD biomarker burden in a cardiovascular care cohort constitutes the empirical foundation for this claim.

The finding is clinically significant for several reasons. First, vascular conditions -- including hypertension, atrial fibrillation, heart failure, and peripheral artery disease -- are established modifiable risk factors for both cognitive decline and Alzheimer disease pathology. This bidirectional relationship means cardiovascular care encounters represent an opportunity to identify at-risk individuals before clinical dementia onset, when disease-modifying or risk-reduction interventions may be most effective. Second, [[hypertensive cerebral small vessel disease affects over half of adults over 65 and is the most modifiable vascular contributor to cognitive decline]], reinforcing that the vascular patient population carries substantial background risk for cognitive impairment that standard cardiology workflows do not capture.

Third, the performance of plasma p-tau217 in this context is clinically relevant. The AUC for detecting cognitive impairment in these cardiovascular patients was 0.94, consistent with [[plasma p-tau217 achieves AUC 94-97 percent for amyloid PET classification in a community cohort enriched for vascular and metabolic comorbidities]], and demonstrating that discriminative accuracy is maintained even when the outcome anchor shifts from amyloid PET to functional cognitive status. This suggests the assay is fit for purpose as a screening instrument in non-neurological care settings.

This claim is an open programmatic recommendation rather than a fully established clinical standard. Critical implementation questions remain unresolved: the optimal screening protocol in cardiovascular settings, which screening instrument to combine with p-tau217, cost-effectiveness relative to alternative screening strategies, and whether early identification in this context changes outcomes. The cost-utility gap is systemic -- [[no plasma AD biomarker has been evaluated for real-world clinical utility including impact on patient outcomes or healthcare resource utilization]] -- meaning the translational recommendation here outpaces the available implementation evidence. The claim is nonetheless supported by the empirical prevalence data and by evidence that the biomarker performs adequately in this population.
