Basic fibroblast growth factor (bFGF) consistently associates with favorable brain aging outcomes: higher bFGF predicts better executive function and higher grey matter volume in both women and men, and higher bFGF distinguishes cognitively normal from MCI groups regardless of amyloid status. bFGF decreases with age (p<0.05) and inversely correlates with vascular risk factor burden in younger individuals.

Beyond angiogenesis, bFGF functions as a neurotrophic factor that attenuates astrocyte activation by reducing GFAP expression. This pleiotropic action -- vascular maintenance plus neuronal resilience plus anti-inflammatory astrocyte modulation -- makes bFGF a candidate protective factor in brain aging.

Connects to [[plasma GFAP is elevated in MCI+AD and LBD but not in FTD or vascular dementia suggesting partial pathway specificity for astrocytic reactivity]] -- bFGF may suppress the astrocytic reactivity that GFAP measures, creating an inverse relationship between bFGF and plasma GFAP.

Connects to [[loss of pericyte coverage causes bbb breakdown and reduced cerebral blood flow which accelerates amyloid-beta accumulation and tau pathology]] -- bFGF's vascular maintenance role may help preserve pericyte coverage.
