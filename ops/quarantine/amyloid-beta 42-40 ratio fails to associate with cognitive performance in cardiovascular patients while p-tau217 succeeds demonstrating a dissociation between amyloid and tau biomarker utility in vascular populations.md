French et al. (2025) enrolled 162 asymptomatic patients referred for coronary angiography or heart failure evaluation -- a population selected on cardiovascular grounds rather than cognitive complaint -- and measured plasma Abeta42/40 and p-tau217 alongside MoCA-based cognitive performance. In multivariate regression adjusting for relevant covariates, the amyloid-beta 42/40 ratio produced no significant coefficient for MoCA score (p>0.05). By contrast, p-tau217 yielded a regression coefficient of beta = -1.46 (p < 0.01) and achieved AUC 0.94 for detecting cognitive impairment on receiver operating characteristic analysis -- [[p-tau217 achieves AUC 0.94 for detecting cognitive impairment in asymptomatic cardiovascular patients]] reports the full diagnostic performance profile for this endpoint.

The null amyloid result is not merely an effect-size disadvantage. The 42/40 ratio dropped out of the model entirely, indicating that amyloid burden -- as captured by this plasma analyte -- carries no independent predictive weight for cognitive status once other variables are entered. Only 10.9% of the cohort had abnormal Abeta42/40 versus 55% with elevated p-tau217, so the low amyloid-positive prevalence in this vascular population may partly explain the null association by restricting range. However, even if low prevalence attenuates effect estimates, the fact that p-tau217 maintains strong discriminative performance (AUC 0.94) under the same sample constraints demonstrates that the asymmetry is not simply a power issue: the tau signal is present and detectable where the amyloid signal is absent. The population-level scale of this discordance -- [[55 percent of asymptomatic cardiovascular patients show elevated p-tau217 indicating high prevalence of occult tau pathology in a non-AD clinical population]] versus only 10.9% amyloid-abnormal -- means the tau-amyloid gap is not a marginal edge case but a feature of this cardiovascular population's biomarker landscape.

This finding extends the established p-tau superiority over amyloid-beta beyond the AD classification literature -- which is anchored on CSF and amyloid PET reference standards in research cohorts -- into a distinct clinical context defined by vascular pathology. The dissociation suggests that in populations where cerebrovascular disease is the primary insult, amyloid accumulation may not be the dominant cognitive driver, whereas tau pathology remains closely coupled to cognitive outcome. This mechanistic interpretation is speculative but consistent with the bidirectional vascular-neurodegenerative pathogenic framework documented elsewhere in this vault.

The implication for biomarker selection in clinical cardiology-adjacent settings is direct: deploying Abeta42/40 as a cognitive risk screen in cardiovascular patients would likely yield no actionable signal, whereas p-tau217 screening could identify patients with occult tau pathology who are at measurable cognitive risk. This dissociation is the biomarker-selection rationale underpinning [[cardiovascular patients represent an underscreened population for AD pathology and cognitive impairment warranting systematic biomarker assessment in cardiovascular care settings]] -- any screening program in this population must anchor on p-tau217, not amyloid-beta, because only the tau analyte carries a detectable signal.
