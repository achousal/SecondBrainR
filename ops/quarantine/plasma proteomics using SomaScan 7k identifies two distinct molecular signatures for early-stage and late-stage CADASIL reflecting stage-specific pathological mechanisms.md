Plasma proteomics studies in CADASIL have tended to treat the disease as a single entity, looking for a unified biomarker panel. Keller et al. 2024 challenge that assumption directly: using SomaScan 7k aptamer-based proteomics on 45 early-stage and 53 late-stage CADASIL patients, they find not one signature but two — and the two do not overlap in any meaningful way.

The early-stage signature (16 proteins) centers on heme biosynthesis and glutathione metabolism: disruption of oxidative homeostasis, implying that what goes wrong first in CADASIL is a metabolic failure at the vascular wall level. The late-stage signature (20 proteins) shifts entirely toward complement dysregulation and lipid-peroxisome metabolism — the classical markers of neuroinflammation and sustained tissue damage.

Because [[heme biosynthesis and glutathione metabolism disruption are the dominant pathway signals in early-stage CADASIL plasma proteomics]] while [[complement dysregulation and lipid-peroxisome metabolism are the dominant pathway signals in late-stage CADASIL plasma proteomics]], the two signatures cannot be collapsed into a single diagnostic or monitoring panel without losing stage-specific information. This has immediate implications: any study that pools CADASIL patients across disease stage and seeks a single plasma biomarker panel is fighting the biology. The signatures are stage-specific by mechanism, not merely by severity.

The distinction also matters therapeutically. Since [[stage-specific plasma protein signatures in CADASIL imply that therapeutic targets differ by disease stage and that a single treatment strategy applied across disease stages is unlikely to be optimal]], the identification of two signatures is not merely an academic subdivision — it is a call for stage-stratified trial design.

The finding is biologically coherent. Early-stage CADASIL is a disease of the vascular wall: endothelial cells and smooth muscle cells begin to fail, NOTCH3 aggregates accumulate, and the metabolic cost of managing this stress appears in the oxidative and heme pathways. Late-stage disease has moved into the parenchyma: white matter lesions are established, neurons and glia are being lost, and the inflammatory machinery has engaged. That the plasma signature tracks this progression suggests it is capturing genuine biology rather than statistical noise.
