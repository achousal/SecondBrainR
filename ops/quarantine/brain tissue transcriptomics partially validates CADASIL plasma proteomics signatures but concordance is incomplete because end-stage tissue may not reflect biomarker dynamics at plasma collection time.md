Brain tissue transcriptomics is the natural next validation step after plasma proteomics: do the proteins elevated in plasma actually show corresponding expression changes in brain tissue? For the CADASIL signatures, Keller 2024 provides this validation, and the answer is: partially, but not cleanly.

Some proteins show expected concordance. HPCAL1 is upregulated in brain tissue (log2FC +1.3), consistent with its elevation in plasma. VEGFR3 is upregulated (+0.7), also consistent. These concordances strengthen the case that the plasma proteins are detecting real brain biology.

But the pattern is not consistent across all proteins. ENPP2 shows a striking inversion: it is downregulated in brain tissue (log2FC -2.6), while it appears as a neuroprotective marker with positive cognition correlations in plasma. This inversion is genuinely puzzling. One interpretation is that ENPP2 is upregulated in oligodendrocytes that are surviving and functioning, while end-stage brain tissue contains predominantly the cells that have failed — so what the brain tissue measurement captures is the absence of ENPP2 in the cells that are already gone, not the signal in the ones still active.

This tension — between plasma and tissue signals — points to a fundamental methodological limitation: all brain tissue in this study is end-stage autopsy material. Cross-sectional plasma samples are collected at various disease stages while patients are alive. The tissue and the plasma are from different points in the disease trajectory, not matched timepoints. Brain tissue at autopsy reflects the endpoint of a process; plasma at the time of blood draw reflects a dynamic ongoing state.

The implication for biomarker validation design is important: [[longitudinal plasma biomarker trajectories differ across dementia subtypes and may provide better discrimination than single-timepoint measurements]], and this applies doubly when comparing against cross-sectional tissue samples. Multi-timepoint plasma collection matched to clinical staging would be needed to resolve whether plasma ENPP2 changes direction as disease progresses to end-stage.
